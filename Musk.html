Flattened Musk Turtle

Sternotherus depressus

An Interactive Guide for Alabama Students

About the Species

This small turtle gets its name from its uniquely flat shell, which helps it hide from predators in narrow underwater rock crevices. It is also known as a "stinkpot" because it can release a foul-smelling musk when threatened.

  • Lifespan: Believed to live up to 50 years.
  • Size: Very small, only growing up to 4-5 inches long.
  • Status in Alabama: Federally listed as Threatened and is found nowhere else in the world.
  • Diet: Primarily eats snails, mussels, and aquatic insects.

Habitat & Range in Alabama

The Flattened Musk Turtle is endemic to Alabama, meaning it's not found anywhere else on Earth. It lives exclusively in the clean, free-flowing streams and rivers of the upper Black Warrior River system.

Its biggest threat is habitat degradation from siltation (cloudy sediment in the water), which smothers their food sources and fills the crevices they use for cover.

This turtle's only home is in parts of this river system.

Movement & Locomotion

As an aquatic turtle, it spends most of its life in the water. Instead of swimming in open water, it typically walks or scurries along the bottom of streams and rivers.

  • They are mostly nocturnal, becoming active at night to hunt for food.
  • During the day, they hide from predators like raccoons and herons under rocks and in crevices.
  • They have a small home range, often living their entire lives in one stretch of a stream.

Nesting Habits

Females leave the water to lay their eggs on land, but they don't travel far from the stream bank.

  • When: Nesting occurs from May through July.
  • Where: They dig shallow nests in soil or leaf litter in partially sunny spots.
  • Eggs: They have very small clutches, laying only 1 to 3 eggs at a time. This low reproductive rate is a reason the species has trouble recovering.

A Different Kind of Tracking

Unlike land tortoises, the Flattened Musk Turtle leaves very few clear footprints. Since it lives on rocky or sandy stream bottoms, its tracks are quickly washed away.

For an AI tracking model, it would be more effective to train the model to identify the turtle itself. The AI could learn to spot its unique flat shape and shell patterns in underwater photos and videos.
