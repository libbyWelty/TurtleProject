Endangered Turtles of Alabama

Loggerhead Sea Turtle (Caretta caretta)

Meet the Loggerhead

The Loggerhead sea turtle is a magnificent marine reptile known for its large head and powerful jaws, which it uses to crush the shells of its prey. It is the most common sea turtle that nests on the shores of Alabama. Despite this, it is listed as a threatened species, facing dangers from pollution, habitat loss, and accidental capture in fishing gear.

These turtles are true ocean wanderers, migrating thousands of miles between their feeding grounds and nesting beaches. Let's dive deeper to learn more about them!

Habitat & Alabama Home

Loggerheads are found in temperate and tropical regions of the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans. They inhabit a wide range of environments during their life, from the open ocean (pelagic) to coastal bays, lagoons, and estuaries.

In Alabama, Loggerheads are primarily found in the coastal waters of the Gulf of Mexico. Their critical habitat includes the nesting beaches of the Fort Morgan Peninsula and the Dauphin Island barrier island system. Organizations like Share the Beach work to protect these nesting areas.

Nesting Habits & Tracks

Nesting season in Alabama runs from May to August. Female loggerheads return to the same beaches where they were born to lay their eggs. Under the cover of darkness, they drag their heavy bodies ashore to dig a nest in the sand with their rear flippers, where they deposit about 100-120 eggs.

Their movement on land is a distinctive crawl, creating a unique track pattern in the sand. The tracks are alternating, comma-shaped marks made by their front flippers, with a wavy tail drag mark down the center. This pattern helps volunteers identify and protect nests.

Identifying Loggerhead Tracks

For your tracking model, it's important to recognize the key features of Loggerhead tracks.

  • Symmetry: The tracks are asymmetrical. The turtle moves its front flippers one at a time, creating an alternating pattern.
  • Flipper Marks: The marks from the front flippers are shaped like commas or parentheses.
  • Width: The track width for an adult female is typically 2.5 to 3 feet wide.
  • Tail Drag: There is usually a distinct, often wavy, line down the middle of the tracks made by the turtle's tail.
