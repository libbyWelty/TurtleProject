Endangered Turtles of Alabama

The Alabama Red-bellied Turtle (Pseudemys alabamensis)

The Alabama Red-bellied Turtle is a large, freshwater turtle that has been Alabama's official state reptile since 1990. It is a federally listed endangered species, found only in the fresh and brackish waters of the Mobile-Tensaw River Delta. Conservation efforts are crucial to protect this unique turtle from threats like habitat loss, pollution, and predation.

Habitat & Range in Alabama

This species thrives in aquatic environments with abundant vegetation, which serves as its primary food source. They prefer slow-moving rivers, streams, and protected bays.

Its entire natural range is confined to the lower part of the Mobile-Tensaw River Delta in Mobile and Baldwin counties. They spend most of their lives in the water but come ashore to bask in the sun on logs and banks, and females travel upland to nest.

Historic range is limited to the Mobile-Tensaw Delta.

Nesting, Movement & Tracks

Nesting Habits

Nesting season typically runs from May through July. The female turtle will leave the water and travel upland to find a suitable spot with open, sandy, and well-drained soil that is exposed to direct sunlight. She digs a flask-shaped nest with her hind feet and lays a clutch of about 4-9 eggs. After laying, she covers the nest, making it difficult to spot.

Locomotion

In water, they are graceful swimmers, using their webbed feet for propulsion. On land, their movement is much slower and more cumbersome as they pull their heavy shells forward.

Identifying Footprints

For your AI model, turtle tracks have distinct features. Look for a central drag mark left by the tail, with comma-shaped claw marks on either side from their feet. The stride (distance between steps) is often short. The exact size depends on the turtle, but the pattern is a key identifier.
