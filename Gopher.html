Gopher Tortoise

Gopherus polyphemus

An Interactive Guide for Alabama Students

About the Species

The Gopher Tortoise is a keystone species, meaning its presence is vital to the health of its ecosystem. It is the only tortoise species found east of the Mississippi River.

  • Lifespan: 40 to 60 years in the wild.
  • Size: 9-15 inches long.
  • Status in Alabama: Federally listed as Threatened in the western counties (Mobile, Washington, Choctaw) and protected by state law elsewhere.
  • Keystone Role: Their burrows provide shelter for over 360 other species, including indigo snakes, gopher frogs, and mice.

Habitat & Range in Alabama

Gopher Tortoises thrive in well-drained, sandy soils, which are easy to dig in. Their preferred habitat is the longleaf pine ecosystem, characterized by open canopies that allow sunlight to reach the forest floor.

In Alabama, they are found in the southern coastal plain counties. The map shows their historical range, with the western populations being under federal protection.

Movement & Burrows

These tortoises are slow-moving land dwellers. Their powerful, shovel-like front legs are perfectly adapted for digging the extensive burrows where they spend up to 80% of their time.

  • Burrows average 15 feet long and 6.5 feet deep.
  • They provide protection from predators, weather extremes, and fire.
  • A tortoise may use several burrows within its home range.
  • The sandy mound at the entrance is called an "apron."

Nesting Habits

Gopher tortoises have very specific nesting needs. The survival rate for eggs and hatchlings is low due to predation.

  • When: Females lay eggs from May to July.
  • Where: They dig a flask-shaped nest in a sunny location, often in the sandy apron of their burrow.
  • Eggs: A clutch contains 5-9 white, round eggs, similar in size to a ping-pong ball.
  • Incubation: The eggs incubate for 80-110 days. The temperature of the sand determines the sex of the hatchlings!

Footprints for Tracking Models

Identifying Gopher Tortoise tracks is key for population studies. The tracks are often found in the sandy soil near their burrow aprons. Your AI model could learn to distinguish these from other animal tracks.

Note the drag marks from the shell (plastron) and the distinct toe impressions.
