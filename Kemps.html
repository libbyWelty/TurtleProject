Endangered Turtles of Alabama

Kemp's Ridley Sea Turtle (Lepidochelys kempii)

Species Overview

The Kemp's Ridley is the smallest and most critically endangered sea turtle species in the world. They are named after Richard Moore Kemp, a fisherman from Key West who first submitted the species for identification in 1906.

  • Average Adult Size: About 2 feet in length.
  • Average Adult Weight: 70-100 pounds.
  • Appearance: Adults have a distinctive round, grayish-green to olive carapace (top shell) and a yellowish-white plastron (bottom shell).
  • Diet: They are primarily carnivores, feeding on crabs (especially blue crabs), clams, mussels, and shrimp.

Habitat

Kemp's Ridleys prefer shallow, coastal waters with sandy or muddy bottoms where their prey is abundant. These areas are known as neritic zones.

While they are most associated with the Gulf of Mexico, juveniles can be found along the Atlantic coast of North America. They spend the vast majority of their lives in the water, with females only coming ashore to nest.

  • Primary Habitat: Gulf of Mexico.
  • Foraging Grounds: Shallow coastal waters, bays, and estuaries.
  • Juvenile Habitat: Can be found in seagrass beds along the Atlantic coast.

Where to Find Them in Alabama

In Alabama, Kemp's Ridley turtles are primarily found in the coastal waters of the Gulf of Mexico. While nesting is extremely rare in Alabama, they are frequently seen foraging in its waters.

  • Key Areas: Mobile Bay, Perdido Bay, and the waters off the coast of Baldwin and Mobile counties.
  • Nesting Sites: While their main nesting beach is in Mexico, very rare and isolated nests have been documented along the Gulf Coast, including the Fort Morgan Peninsula and Dauphin Island in Alabama.
  • Conservation Note: They are often seen by fishermen and boaters, and conservation efforts are crucial to protect their foraging grounds in Alabama.

Unique Nesting Habits

Kemp's Ridleys are famous for their unique, synchronized nesting behavior called an "arribada," which means "arrival" in Spanish. Thousands of females come ashore at the same time during daylight hours to lay their eggs.

  • Primary Nesting Beach: Rancho Nuevo, Tamaulipas, Mexico. Over 95% of all nesting occurs here.
  • Nesting Season: Primarily from April to July.
  • Clutch Size: About 100 eggs per nest. Females may nest 2-3 times per season.
  • Incubation Period: Approximately 45 to 60 days. The sex of the hatchlings is determined by the temperature of the sand.

Movement and Locomotion

Sea turtles are adapted for life in the ocean, but their movement on land is slow and laborious.

In Water

Kemp's Ridleys are powerful swimmers. They use their long front flippers for propulsion in a motion similar to a bird's flapping wings. Their rear flippers act as rudders for steering. They are capable of migrating long distances between foraging and nesting grounds.

On Land

On land, their movement is much different. They use a symmetrical gait, moving both front flippers forward at the same time, then both rear flippers. This creates a distinctive track pattern in the sand as they drag their bodies forward. This is different from loggerhead turtles, which use an alternating gait.

Identifying Footprints

Identifying turtle tracks is key to finding nests for conservation. The symmetrical gait of the Kemp's Ridley leaves a unique pattern.

Track Characteristics

  • Pattern: Parallel, evenly spaced marks from the front flippers pushing into the sand at the same time.
  • Tail Drag Mark: A central line down the middle of the tracks, though it can be faint.
  • Width: The track width is relatively narrow, typically 25-30 inches, corresponding to the turtle's small size.
  • Flipper Marks: The marks look like commas or crescent shapes in the sand.

This symmetrical track is a key differentiator from other turtles like Loggerheads, which leave alternating, staggered flipper marks.
